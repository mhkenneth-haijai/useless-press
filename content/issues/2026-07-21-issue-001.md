This is the inaugural issue of *Useless Press* — a magazine we started as a kind of notebook for things we couldn't stop thinking about. The first thing on our desk right now is a small black-and-white camera. It costs as much as a used motorcycle. It cannot take colour photographs. We have been arguing about it for two weeks.

We present here our finding. Three quiet features, one brief roundup, and one follow-up.

## How a Monochrome Sensor Sees

> "Each pixel of the monochrome-dedicated sensor captures the subject's brightness information."

To understand what a Ricoh GR IV Monochrome does, you have to first understand what every other modern digital camera does.

A colour CMOS sensor is, beneath its marketing, a greyscale sensor with a coloured mosaic laid over it. On every four photosites — two greens, one red, one blue — a thin colour filter blocks most of the light and lets through only a slice. The camera's processor then *guesses* what colour the missing two-thirds of each pixel was, by interpolating between neighbours. The result, with twenty years of engineering behind it, is convincing. It is also, by definition, a lie.

A monochrome sensor strips the mosaic away. No filter. Every photosite receives every photon. At the silicon level, this is a sensor that *wants* to do greyscale, and has been left alone to do it. Each pixel is 1:1 mapped to the brightness it recorded. No interpolation. No demosaic rounding. No guessing.

![Diagram comparing a colour-CFA sensor's pixel array to a monochrome sensor's — the latter receives all wavelengths directly, producing a true per-pixel luminance reading.](https://v3b.fal.media/files/b/0aa30a40/PzjnYoXQfTgSx9ovh-p73_wolj15jf.png)

Three consequences matter for a photographer holding a $2,199 Ricoh in their hands.

The first is **per-pixel sharpness**. Because there is no demosaic step, the recorded image has no row-and-column patterns of softness. The 25.7 megapixel count on the GR IV Mono is the *honest* pixel count: each pixel resolves real detail, not interpolated detail. Reviews will show the difference as a quiet, almost architectural cleanliness — no false texture, no micro-blur.

The second is **tonal gradation**. A colour sensor turning B&W asks: how bright? how red, green, blue? — losing sensor bit depth to colour accounting. A monochrome sensor can spend every bit on luminance. The result in a strong scene — a closed door, a tiled roof under winter sun — is the ability to keep both highlight and shadow detail that a converted colour image would have crushed.

The third is **high-ISO character**. Photons that would have been blocked by the colour filter are now collected. The GR IV Mono's *Standard Output ISO range* runs to 409,600 — a number that, on a colour sensor, would be marketing nonsense, but on a real luminance sensor is closer to something the camera can actually deliver. Gain becomes not noise *amplification* but signal *collection*.

It is not strictly a better sensor than a colour equivalent. It is a sensor *built for one job* and frankly refusing any others. That refusal, at $2,199, is the bet you are paying for.

## What 262 Grams of Black Can Do

> "The GR series has consistently remained committed to the essential values of a camera – high image quality, quick response and portability."

Ricoh has been making the GR *the same shape* since the film GR1 of 1996. That is thirty years of a single industrial design intention: a rectangle small enough to vanish into a jacket, with a lens precise enough that — when raised — does not embarrass you.

The numbers are humble and exact. **109.4 × 61.1 × 32.7 mm.** **228 grams body only**, 262 with battery and SD. The lens protrudes 31.3 mm when extended; otherwise it is a flat brick that can sit in the back pocket of a pair of jeans. There is no grip to speak of — a small, raised thumb rest on the back, a faint front finger profile, a strap lug where a thin leather wrist strap can hang.

This is not a camera body you write home about. It is one you write home *with*.

![GR IV Mono shown from the rear, displaying the top dial, the Fn button, and the rear thumb rest layout that has barely changed since the GR DIGITAL III of 2009.](https://ricohgr.eu/cdn/shop/files/GR-4-MONO-2_1920x.jpg?v=1775040250)

What is new in this generation, and particular to the monochrome variant:

The top-left dial has been reworked for the new Image Control options: dedicated monochrome *modes* rather than colour filters, named things like **Monochrome Hard**, **Monochrome Soft**, **Monochrome IR-cut** (Ricoh plays coy with names in the press materials — the actual firmware names will reveal themselves once the camera reaches reviewers' hands).

The Fn button — already a GR tradition — is now *indexed to the red filter*. Pressing it inside shooting mode toggles a built-in physical red filter effect. This is not the same as a JPEG processing filter: it is an actual filter placed in front of the lens via a tiny mechanical actuator, mimicking the darkroom red filter that B&W film photographers have been shoving in front of their lenses since 1925. Sky tones darken, foliage goes heavy, skin lightens. For street portraiture under bright sun, it is the cheat code that pays for the camera.

The 5-axis IBIS — sensor-shift shake reduction, 6 stops compensation — is the same unit the GR IV uses, inherited without modification. The battery life is unchanged: 250 shots CIPA, which is to say roughly a day of real street shooting if you keep the rear screen off and use the optical-style snap focus.

What is *not* new, and matters: the menu. The GR menu is famously dense, occasionally cryptic, full of small knobs and dials that long-time GR owners have learned to live in. A new owner will spend an afternoon with it and emerge somewhat reluctantly fluent. This is not a complaint; this is a tradition. The Q2 does this too.

## Three Cameras You Should Compare It To (and Three You Shouldn't)

When you take a $2,199 camera that has *deliberately* lost the ability to do the thing that 99% of modern cameras exist to do, you have to ask: **what else am I paying for, and what am I giving up?**

### The honest comparisons

#### The Leica Q2 Monochrome — the rightful king, from a distance

The Q2 Mono is what every other monochrome compact aspires to be. Full-frame, 47.3 megapixels, a Summilux 28mm f/1.7, $5,995. The Ricoh is what it aspires to be *from the other end*: APS-C, 25.7 megapixels, a 28mm-equivalent f/2.8, $2,199. The two cameras share a 28mm focal philosophy, a similar small-detachable-lens feel, and a philosophical commitment to native monochrome.

What the Leica has that the Ricoh doesn't:

- A full stop and a half of aperture (f/1.7 vs f/2.8 — meaningful at night)
- A full-frame sensor (more dynamic range, more recovery in post)
- Twice the resolution (47 vs 25 MP)
- Optical construction that is, frankly, in a different tier

What the Ricoh has that the Leica doesn't:

- 36% of the price
- A third of the weight (262g vs 734g)
- A pocket that closes over it

If you are a Leica buyer, this is not your camera. If you are a Leica-leaning photographer who cannot quite afford it, this is your camera. The gap between them is closer than the spec sheets suggest.

#### The Pentax K-3 Mark III Monochrome — the sensor twin, in a different body

The Pentax K-3 III Mono uses — by strong likelihood — the same generation 25.7 megapixel monochrome APS-C sensor as the GR IV Mono. Same resolution. Same era. Ricoh Imaging makes both cameras (Pentax being a sister brand under the Ricoh Imaging corporate umbrella).

The Pentax is a DSLR with a K-mount; the Ricoh is a fixed-lens compact. They are not directly comparable — one is a system camera, the other is a single-purpose instrument. But the *image quality* you get from each should be within a margin of error. A photographer who already owns Pentax glass and wants native B&W should buy the K-3 III Mono. A photographer starting fresh, or already committed to a small camera, should look at the GR IV Mono first.

#### The Fuji X100VI — the comparison everyone will make, that doesn't really apply

The X100VI is the most obvious point of reference at the $1,599–$1,799 price tier. It is also a totally different category. The Fuji shoots colour first and *simulates* black and white via film sims (ACROS is, and remains, the best B&W simulation in any modern camera). The Ricoh shoots B&W at the silicon level.

These cameras produce different *kinds* of B&W. The Fuji's ACROS is gorgeous — tonally rich, contrast-tuned, instantly interpretable. The Ricoh's monochrome is *raw*: cleaner highlight rolloff, sharper pixel-level detail, more headroom in shadows. If you have been satisfied with Fuji's ACROS, the Ricoh is not a clear upgrade; it is a different aesthetic. The deciding factor is whether you want a *tool that does one job* or a *tool that does many jobs and one of them really well*.

### The dishonest comparisons

#### The iPhone with a B&W filter

Yes, the iPhone shoots 48 megapixels of monochrome via its Monochrome mode. No, that is not what this camera is competing with. It is competing with what you would do *with your phone* once you decide to take photography seriously enough to spend $2,199 — and with the question of whether the $2,199 buys you something the phone cannot.

#### A monochrome Leica M11

The M11 Monochrome is $8,995. The M-mount rangefinder experience is its own canon — half tool, half liturgical object. If you already own M glass, the M11 Mono is the natural choice. If you do not, the M-mount system is a long, expensive road that has nothing to do with this $2,199 compact.

#### The Ricoh GR IV HDF

The colour version of this camera is roughly the same price. If you want monochrome *occasionally* and flexibility often, the HDF is the tool. The Mono is the camera for someone who has decided they are photographing in greyscale for the foreseeable future, and would rather not pay for the circuitry they do not need.

### The honest verdict

The Ricoh GR IV Monochrome is not the right camera for everyone. It is not even the right camera for most people who take black-and-white photographs. It is the right camera for the person who has been waiting for it — the GR user, the street photographer, the documentary worker — who has weighed the colour options and now wants an instrument built for the single thing they keep doing.

## Other Monochrome Things

Three pieces of news across the wider monochrome world that we noticed while writing this issue. None of them are as consequential as the Ricoh — but each is interesting in its own small way.

* **Leica's slow drift toward monochrome film.** The Leica recently introduced a 35mm Cine Still BW film in their ongoing specialty film range; we are not convinced anyone outside of hardcore Leica collectors will shoot it, but the existence of the offering signals that the segment is, at minimum, monetisable.
* **Yashica's quiet comeback in Japan.** The newly revived Yashica has been working on a film SLR with a fixed 45mm lens and leaf shutter that, while primarily marketed to chromatic shooters, has a Mono-joint firmware preference to bypass the colour metering cell — a circuit that requires filing for a service centre visit. We will report on this once we have actually held one.
* **The ever-present Fuji rumour.** Fuji Rumors continues to insist that a monochrome X-Pro or X80 is imminent. We have heard this rumour since 2022. We will stop repeating it until Fuji makes the announcement themselves, because we have been here four times and the ground has yet to shift.

One we are closing the door on: software-only monochrome workflows (Silver Efex Pro, RNI Films, VSCO B&W). They remain excellent. None of them are this kind of camera.

## What We Got Wrong Last Year

Twelve months ago, in a private note we keep meaning to publish, we predicted:

1. **Fujifilm would announce a monochrome X-Pro by mid-2026.** *Unconfirmed.* As of this writing, Fuji Rumors still publishes quarterly items titled "Where is the monochrome Fuji?" The camera has not appeared.
2. **Sony would not enter the dedicated-monochrome market in 2026.** *Confirmed correct.* Sony continues to issue high-resolution colour sensors and let B&W live in the Sony A7R V JPEG menu.
3. **Someone would build a monochrome compact camera under $1,500 by end of 2026.** *Probably wrong.* The Ricoh GR IV Monochrome, the natural candidate, is $2,199. The Pentax K-3 III Mono, the other candidate, hovers near $2,400. The under-$1,500 monochrome compact remains hypothetical.

So much for predictions. We will check in again at the end of 2026.
